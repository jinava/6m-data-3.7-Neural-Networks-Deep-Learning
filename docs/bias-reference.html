1a · The deep version — "Bias, harder"

The deepest truth: bias is what makes a layer affine, not linear — and affine is the whole game. A linear map obeys one ironclad rule: f(0) = 0. Compose linear maps and they keep fixing the origin. The part people miss: this stays true even with nonlinearities, if there are no biases — a ReLU/tanh network with every bias removed satisfies N(0) = 0 at every depth (because f(0) = 0). It literally cannot output anything but zero from the zero vector. The nonlinearity gives you bending (the XOR story); the bias gives you placement. Algebraically a neuron applies an affine map then a nonlinearity, and the affine group splits into a linear part (the weight matrix — rotation/scale/shear) and a translation (the bias). Weights rotate; bias translates.

The crown jewel: in log-odds space, bias is the prior. Logistic regression (= one perceptron) computes log( P(y=1|x) / P(y=0|x) ) = b + w·x. Here w·x is the evidence (the log-likelihood-ratio from the features) and b is the log prior odds — what you'd believe before seeing any feature. So z = w·x + b is exactly posterior log-odds = prior + evidence, and the sigmoid just converts back to a probability. That is why the correct output bias on an imbalanced task is log(p/(1−p)) for base rate p. Deep nets are not doing literal Bayes per neuron, but the structure carries: bias is each unit's standing lean before its inputs vote.

The insight with teeth: bias is the life-support of a ReLU unit. A ReLU neuron is max(0, w·x + b). Whether any gradient flows back through it depends on whether w·x + b > 0, and bias is the dial that sets that. Strongly negative bias → the unit is silent on nearly every input → output 0, gradient 0, no learning → a dead neuron. This is why a known init for deep ReLU nets sets biases to a small positive constant (≈0.01) rather than 0.

Why bias secretly couples to your data's mean. If inputs are not centered, bias and weights become coupled in the loss landscape (nudging a weight shifts the mean pre-activation, which the bias must chase) — worse conditioning, slower training. BatchNorm's learnable shift β is just a bias relocated to after the normalization — which is why you use bias=False on the layer before a BatchNorm: the norm already subtracts the mean and re-adds its own offset.

Unifying one-liner: across affine geometry, Bayesian priors, ReLU survival, and optimization conditioning, bias is the one parameter that never touches the input. Weights answer "how do I revise my answer given evidence?"; bias answers "what is my answer with no evidence?" — the network's anchor / prior / floor / translation, all at once.

1b · The plain-English story

Picture a tiny gatekeeper at a door whose whole job is to answer "Yes or no?" — will this customer check out? People walk up and hand her clues ("the cart has 3 items", "they're a returning customer"). How much she trusts each clue is the weights.

But before a single person shows up, she already has a mood — a default lean. Maybe she is an optimist who assumes "yes" unless given a reason to doubt; maybe a skeptic who assumes "no" until truly convinced. That starting lean — her answer when the room is empty — is the bias.

Why does she need one? A gatekeeper forced to start perfectly neutral every time could never say "around here most people actually DO buy, so I'll lean yes." The real world is not centered on zero — most emails are not spam, most visitors do not buy. Bias lets her hold a sensible default that matches how the world really leans. A nice way to feel it: bias is the bar you have to clear — a skeptic sets it high (you need a mountain of good clues), an optimist sets it low.

Where did her lean come from? Nobody handed her the perfect attitude. She started with a near-blank one (the system gives a small random/zero starting mood), then learned it on the job — every wrong answer nudged it — until it settled into the honest base rate of the place. You did not set it; the data taught her.

And the deepest part: on the day the clues stop coming — every input zero — what is left? Only her lean. Bias is the part of her judgment that does not depend on anyone walking through the door.

In one breath: weights = what the neuron learns from the world; bias = what it believes before the world speaks. Both are learned, but only bias survives the silence. A deep network is just millions of these gatekeepers stacked in layers, each carrying its own quiet bias.

1c · Technical note (engineering brief, v2)

Summary. A standard neuron computes output = f( (inputs · weights) + bias ). The inner part z = (inputs · weights) + bias is the pre-activation; the activation f then produces the output. Weights scale each input; bias is one learned scalar per neuron that sets its baseline before the inputs are weighed. Bias is learned from data (not configured) and is optional (sometimes switched off).

1. One neuron. z = (x₁·w₁ + … + xₙ·wₙ) + b (a scalar); output = f(z). If all inputs are 0, z = b and the output is f(b) — bias is the neuron's baseline before any input. Same role as the intercept in y = mx + b.

2. One neuron vs a layer (scalar vs vector). A layer of N neurons computes z = W·x + b (PyTorch: y = xAᵀ + b):

one neuron layer of N neurons
weighted sum z scalar vector (length N)
bias b scalar vector (N — one per neuron)
weights row of n matrix N×n

A layer does not have "a bias" — it has one bias per neuron, stored as a vector (PyTorch bias shape (out_features,); scikit-learn intercepts_[i]).

3. Why it exists. (a) Removes the "boundary must pass through 0" constraint — without bias a neuron cannot even learn "flag when value > 5". (b) Lets each neuron match a real-world baseline (most emails are not spam). (c) It is the network's intercept. Intuition: weights say how to react to evidence; bias says what to assume before any evidence.

4. Lifecycle & range. No fixed default value: you choose only whether it exists (bias=True/False); the framework sets the initial number (PyTorch: ±1/√(in_features); from-scratch: often 0); training learns the final value from data. Range: bounded at init, any real number afterwards.

5. Operational notes. Dead ReLU units come from too-negative bias (init near 0 / slightly positive). Output-layer bias ≈ base rate — init to log(p/(1−p)) on imbalanced data. BatchNorm makes bias redundant → bias=False before it. Bias is normally excluded from weight decay.

References (official, verified): nn.Linear (y = xAᵀ + b, bias (out_features,), init ±1/√(in_features)) · MLPClassifier.intercepts_ · CS231n neuron model

2 · How bias is handled in the code (cross-notebook survey)

The L07 notebooks handle bias three different ways. Everything below maps to real cells.

Regime Where Who manages bias
scikit-learn (LogisticRegression, MLPClassifier) nb01, nb02, nb04 hidden in the estimator (intercept_ / intercepts_)
PyTorch nn.Linear (and nn.Sequential) nb03, nb04, assignment, optional auto-created tensor, framework-initialised, optimiser-learned
Hand-rolled NumPy optional_extensions §1 you declare, init, differentiate and update it yourself

1. Defined / created

  • PyTorch nn.Linear(in, out) auto-creates a bias tensor of shape (out,) because the default is bias=True. nb03 SimpleMLP → biases 16+1 = 17 of 177 params; nb04 MLP → 32+32+1 = 65 of 1,409.
  • sklearn MLPClassifier stores biases in model.intercepts_ (one vector per layer); no off-switch.
  • sklearn LogisticRegression: bias = intercept_, governed by fit_intercept=True.
  • NumPy from scratch: you declare it — b1 = np.zeros(hidden), b2 = np.zeros(1).

2. Initial value assigned

  • Framework default: PyTorch nn.Linear seeds each bias from U(−√k, √k), k = 1/in_features (≈ ±1/√(in_features)) — small random, not zero.
  • Explicit: the NumPy net uses np.zeros; the weight-init demo (optional §2) calls nn.init.zeros_(m.bias). Zero-init is safe for bias but the all-zeros weight init is broken.

3. Forward formula

  • One neuron: z = (Σ xᵢ·wᵢ) + b (scalar); output = f(z).
  • Layer, matrix form — exactly as the NumPy net codes it: z1 = X @ W1 + b1. X@W1 is (N, hidden), b1 is (hidden,), and broadcasting adds the same bias vector to every row. PyTorch nn.Linear: y = xAᵀ + b.

4. Update (the learning) — the gradient

The hand-coded backward pass (optional §1) is the clearest: - db2 = dp.sum(axis=0) and db1 = dz1.sum(axis=0) — the bias gradient is the sum of the upstream gradient over the batch, because ∂z/∂b = 1 (so ∂L/∂b = Σ ∂L/∂z). The weight gradient dW = X.T @ dz multiplies the inputs back in; bias does not. - Update: b1 -= lr * db1. In PyTorch this is automatic — bias is in model.parameters(), so loss.backward() fills bias.grad and optimizer.step() applies it (SGD: b ← b − lr·db; Adam: same gradient, per-parameter adaptive step).

5. Counting

  • PyTorch sum(p.numel() for p in model.parameters()) ✅ counts weights and biases.
  • sklearn sum(c.size for c in coefs_) ❌ counts weights only — biases are in intercepts_.

6. One-line summary per regime

Aspect scikit-learn PyTorch nn.Linear NumPy from scratch
define automatic automatic (bias=True) b = np.zeros(...)
init value internal U(−√k,√k), k=1/in_features 0
forward internal y = xAᵀ + b z = X@W + b (broadcast)
gradient internal autograd db = dz.sum(axis=0)
update internal solver optimizer.step() b -= lr*db
count intercepts_/intercept_ in model.parameters() you track it

3 · What to take care of — and the correct fixes

Functionally the bias handling is sound — the forward + b, the gradient db = dz.sum, and the optimiser updates are all correct. The items below are one mislabelled number and three best-practice tighten-ups.

#1 — Incorrect: parameter count omits biases (nb02)

The sklearn-MLP cell prints sum(c.size for c in model.coefs_) = 816, labelled "# parameters". coefs_ is weights only; the true count for 32→16 is 865 (816 weights + 49 biases). Cosmetic (a diagnostic print), but mislabelled.

n_weights = sum(c.size for c in model.coefs_)        # 816
n_biases  = sum(b.size for b in model.intercepts_)   # 49
print(f"# parameters: {n_weights + n_biases:,}")     # 865

#2 — Take care: weight_decay also decays biases (nb04, Extension 4)

optim.Adam(m.parameters(), lr=lr, weight_decay=wd) applies the L2 penalty to every parameter, including biases (the Adam docs expose one scalar; excluding bias needs param groups). Decaying a bias pulls the model off the data's true baseline. Production-correct:

decay    = [p for n, p in m.named_parameters() if p.ndim > 1]   # weight matrices
no_decay = [p for n, p in m.named_parameters() if p.ndim <= 1]  # biases + norm params
opt = optim.Adam(
    [{"params": decay, "weight_decay": wd},
     {"params": no_decay, "weight_decay": 0.0}],
    lr=lr,
)

#3 — Take care: redundant bias before BatchNorm (optional §4)

MLPWithRegularisation puts nn.Linear(n_features, hidden) straight before nn.BatchNorm1d(hidden) with bias=True. BatchNorm subtracts the mean (erasing the bias) and re-adds its own shift β, so the Linear bias is dead weight. Fix:

layers = [nn.Linear(n_features, hidden, bias=not use_bn)]   # bias=False when BatchNorm follows
if use_bn:
    layers.append(nn.BatchNorm1d(hidden))

#4 — Opportunity: seed the output bias on imbalanced data

Every PyTorch model starts the output bias near zero. The output bias is the prior log-odds, so on a skewed target, initialising it to the base rate starts the model at the prior instead of discovering it:

import math
p = float(y_tr.mean())                       # base rate
with torch.no_grad():
    model.layers[-1].bias.fill_(math.log(p / (1 - p)))

#5 — Minor: bias lives on the model's device

model.to(device) moves bias tensors too. Always move the whole model, never individual tensors, or you risk a device-mismatch error between weights and biases.
